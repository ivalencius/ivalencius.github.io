Satellite Derived Suspended Sediment Measurements

The dynamics of river suspended sediment, derived from soil erosion, are critical for understanding floodplain and coastal wetland evolution, as well as reservoir sedimentation. Although the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) has conducted over 100k suspended sediment concentration (SSC) sampling campaigns, data availability is often sparse or altogether lacking for large river transects. Landsat derived SSC measurements have proven accurate enough to supplement USGS datasets, allowing unprecedented spatial analysis of SSC trends throughout large river systems (Dethier et al., 2020). Here, we build on this approach by applying it to higher spatial and temporal resolution datasets, namely the Sentinel-2 satellite. Through a cluster and regression approach, we have constrained the uncertainty of Sentinel-2 derived SSC to less than a factor of two, which has proven adequate for large rivers. In combination with the Landsat record, this allows for a multidecadal analysis of sediment transport dynamics across multi-dammed systems. We apply these methods to the Chattahoochee River in Georgia and Alabama, USA. Using observations from 1984 to 2022, we find pronounced decreases in SSC downstream of dams along the river, with downstream reaches never regaining the same values as upstream observations. We also see a decreasing trend in SSC across the whole river reach, which could be indicative of changing land-use practices.

Other Projects

Classification-4u
During my junior year I took a class in biomedical image analysis. This course focused on using machine learning methods in a variety of ways for medical imagery. The same sort of tasks show up in both medicine and the environmental sciences, namely semantic segmentation - the assignment of a label to every pixel in an image. The Snyder lab does this often with historical orthophotos to create records of logging and human development over time. This process is time intensive so I wanted to see if I could come up with a way to bring automatic segmentation pipelines to the environmental sciences.

I identified technical and hardware limitations as the two primary drivers keeping machine learning inaccessible to many people. Zero Cost Deep Learning 4 Microscopy is an amazing toolbox implemented in Google Colab, which allows any Google user access to a GPU (crucial for machine learning). Implemented as an intuitive GUI, ZeroCostDL4Mic allows those without coding experience to use high-power tensorflow models. My independent project focused on altering this toolbox for use with satellite imagery. This took the form of the following changes:

1. Implementing caching so images of arbitrarily large size can be used.
2. Implementing a notebook for RGB imagery (can be edited to work with any number of bands).
3. Integrating the notebook with segmentation_models, a library that provides 4 separate model architectures with 25 different backbones.
4. Integrating the notebook with Smooth Blending. Blending is not as important for smaller microscopy images but is vital for increasing the accuracy and usability of larger land cover maps.
